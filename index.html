About

I'm a first-year M.S. student in Applied Mathematics at San Diego State University, with a B.S. in Mechanical Engineering from Cal Poly SLO. My path into applied math grew directly out of engineering practice: working on air quality compliance at SCS Engineers showed me how much real environmental problems depend on getting the underlying models right, and I wanted the mathematical depth to do that well.

My work now sits at the intersection of dynamical systems, computational PDEs, and scientific computing. I'm especially drawn to problems where physical intuition and rigorous analysis inform each other — whether that's equation discovery from noisy data, environmental transport modeling, or the structure of nonlinear systems. I bring an engineer's instinct for what matters in practice alongside the mathematical tools to dig into why.

Wind Turbine Rotor Design & Testing

Designed and fabricated a three-bladed wind turbine rotor for competition at the Cal Poly Wind Power Research Center. Applied blade element momentum theory to compute chord and twist distributions, then manufactured the blades from PVC using heat-forming techniques to approximate airfoil camber within tight material and budget constraints. The rotor successfully generated measurable power in field testing. The gap between predicted and measured performance prompted analysis of blade-to-blade consistency and the aerodynamic cost of fixed pitch — the kind of modeling question that pushed me toward applied mathematics.

Portable Ice Rink — Thermal Systems Design

Designed a portable refrigerated ice rink for Cal Poly Open House, modeling the full thermal system from heat load to hardware selection. Built a 2D finite difference conduction model in Excel to determine steady-state temperature distributions across ice, insulation, and concrete layers, and used Engineering Equation Solver to size PEX tube spacing, refrigerant temperatures, and flow rates for both an 8-hour and 24-hour freeze cycle. Selected pump and chiller hardware based on system curves and operating point analysis, and produced a full cost estimate for both designs.

Chaos and Localization Phenomena in the Discrete Nonlinear Schrödinger Lattice

Used the Discrete Nonlinear Schrödinger Lattice to show how a system governed by a simple nonlinear recurrence can generate intricate spatial structures through purely geometric mechanisms. Did so by:

  • Recasting the steady state Discrete Nonlinear Schrödinger lattice equation to a 2D map

  • Analyzing how localized modes, periodic structures, and chaotic spatial patterns arise from invariant manifolds

  • Examining bright and dark solitons leading to heteroclinic and homoclinic orbits of the map, respectively

  • Conducting a bifurcation analysis that shows the onset of chaotic behavior.

Modeling the Impact of Temperature and Rainfall on Dengue Fever Occurences

This project extends an existing temperature-driven dengue transmission model by adding seasonal rainfall effects on mosquito habitat and survival, creating a more complete picture of how climate drives disease spread. Using coupled differential equations, the model simulates mosquito and human infection dynamics, finding that epidemic outbreaks closely follow peaks in rainfall and mosquito abundance. Validated against climate and dengue data from Nepal, the model demonstrates how joint temperature-rainfall variability generates the recurring seasonal epidemic waves observed in real-world dengue transmission.

ODE Analysis of Odell's 1980 Predator Prey Model

Analyzed the Odell predator-prey model from Strogatz's Nonlinear Dynamics and Chaos using qualitative phase-plane methods, focusing on nullclines, equilibrium stability, and how system behavior shifts with a parameter. Analytically, the model predicts transcritical and Hopf bifurcations. These transitions are illustrated through numerical phase portraits, time series, and trajectory plots.
